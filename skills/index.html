We would also like you to have experience with two or three CAD (computer aided design) modeling to fabrication workflows, including laser cutting, waterjet cutting, and 3d printing. You should also have basic experience with measuring, marking, and layout work, an intuition for tolerance in various processes, and to understand the difference between precision and accuracy. Acquisition of these skills can be accomplished either through the prototyping working that you do for the class project, or through a short exercise called *making a box three ways,* in which you will produce a small box via the laser cutter, a second small box in sheet metal, and a third that is 3D printed. If you have experience in some or all of these areas, documenting that experience through prototyping for the class project may be the most efficient way to complete this requirement. If you have little or no prior experience, you may be able to complete everything through prototyping, but should also consider proactively taking on the *box three ways* assignment as a means to acquire skills that will help you to make prototypes for the class project more efficiently. The deliverable for the skills checklist is a page on the course website showing a photo of the each of the following: * Layout work including measuring and marking for manual fabrication * Use of a hand drill * Use of a drill press * Use of a bandsaw * Laser cutting * Screenshots of a CAD model * Photos of the laser cutting process * Photo of final product produced with a laser cutter * 3D printing * Screenshot of a CAD model * Screenshot of .stl file * Screenshot of 3d printer toolpaths * Photo of a 3d printed component * Sheetmetal workflow * Photo or screenshot of layout (can be CAD or by hand) * Photo of cutting process (can be waterjet or manual) * Photos of deburring and post-processing steps * Photo of assembled box * Screenshot of CAD assembly modeled that includes parts you've modeled mated with external geometry such as hardware from McMaster-Carr Please do satisfy multiple requirements in one step when possible, and label accordingly (eg. CAD assembly model shown in 3d printing exercise, etc.) ### Resources: **CAD:** We are planning to support two CAD platforms, [Fusion 360](https://www.autodesk.com/products/fusion-360/overview) by Autodesk, and [Solidworks](https://www.solidworks.com/) by Dassault Systemes. If you have never used CAD before or you do not run Windows on your primary computer, Fusion 360 is probably the place to start. The first step will be to create an [education account](https://www.autodesk.com/education/students), then follow the install instructions. In addition to supporting Windows and MacOS, Fusion has the advantage of being cloud-based, so collaboration is more streamlined. Fusion 360 has a large and active [user community](https://forums.autodesk.com/t5/fusion-360/ct-p/1234), and many [official tutorials](https://help.autodesk.com/view/fusion360/ENU/courses/#3d-modeling), such as this [_creating a sheet metal component_](https://help.autodesk.com/view/fusion360/ENU/courses/AP-CREATING-SHEET-METAL-COMPONENT) tutorial that you may find helpful. There are also numerous unofficial tutorials and learning resources available, such as this [YouTube Channel](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCo29kn3d9ziFUZGZ50VKvWA). Solidworks is oriented more towards professional engineering applications, whereas Fusion 360 aims to be accessible to hobbyists, though the learning curves are not too different. Learning Solidworks will be a great investment of time if you think your professional work in the future might require it, in which case you can install the [student version from MIT IS&amp;T](https://ist.mit.edu/solidworks/2021/student), and if you already use Solidworks you are welcome to continue using it for the class. Once you have Solidworks installed, you can access a fairly comprehensive set of [built-in tutorials](./solidworks-tutorials.jpg) through the help menu. **Make a box:** If you wish, feel free to start your laser cut box exercise using either [MakerCase](https://en.makercase.com/#/) or [make a box.io](https://makeabox.io/), both of which can generate a drawing that can be used to laser cut a box. It's not necessary, strictly speaking, to model a box in 3d and CAD and then create a drawing, at this point, though that is a valuable exercise if you haven't done it before. If you do choose to use files from either of these websites as a starting point, though, please customize the box in some way (add your initials?) before sending it to the laser cutter.
